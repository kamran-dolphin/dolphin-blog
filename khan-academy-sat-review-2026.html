Khan Academy SAT Prep Review 2026: Is It Still Worth It?

Khan Academy has been the go-to free SAT resource since 2015. And honestly, for good reason. Official College Board partnership, thousands of practice questions, zero cost. Hard to argue with that.

But the SAT has changed a lot since then. It's fully digital now, adaptive, and way shorter. So the question is: does Khan still hold up in 2026?

What Khan Gets Right

The content is genuinely solid. Video explanations are clear, practice questions align with College Board material, and the diagnostic helps you figure out where you're weak. For something completely free, it's impressive.

The personalized recommendations based on your PSAT scores give you a real starting point. And parents trust the brand, which means less friction getting started.

If you're a self-motivated learner who can sit down for a structured study session, Khan works great.

Where Khan Falls Short in 2026

The biggest problem is the format. Khan was built for desktop study sessions. Sit down, watch a video, grind through 20 problems, review. But let's be real: over 65% of students prefer studying on their phones in short bursts between classes. Khan's mobile app exists, but it wasn't built mobile-first. It feels like a desktop site squeezed onto a smaller screen.

The second problem is motivation. Khan relies entirely on you deciding to sit down and study. No meaningful streaks, no social pressure, nothing that makes practice feel less like homework. For the top 20% of students who are naturally disciplined, that's fine. For everyone else? They open Khan once, do 10 questions, and never come back.

Third: the Digital SAT itself is adaptive, but Khan's practice experience doesn't really simulate that. It still feels like the old SAT format.

The Gap Khan Leaves Open

Khan is great at teaching concepts. Where it struggles is building the daily practice habit that actually moves your score.

The research here is pretty clear: practicing a little bit every day beats cramming on weekends. A student who does 15 minutes daily for 8 weeks will almost always outscore someone who does 3-hour sessions on Saturdays, even if the Saturday student logs more total hours.

But Khan's format works against quick daily sessions. Opening the app, picking a topic, loading a video, navigating through problems... there's too much friction for a 10-minute session on the bus.

What Students Are Switching To

A newer wave of SAT apps addresses this gap directly. Apps like Dolphin deliver questions in a TikTok-style swipeable feed. Each question stands alone, sessions can be 2 minutes, and the whole thing matches how students already use their phones.

The numbers back this up: Dolphin's internal data shows 90.5% retention for users who complete a daily goal, compared to single-digit retention for users who don't build the habit.

It's not that one platform is "better." It's that they solve different problems.

The Best Strategy: Use Both

Here's what the ideal SAT prep stack looks like in 2026:

  • Khan Academy for learning concepts and watching video explanations
  • A daily practice app like Dolphin for building consistency and retention
  • Official College Board practice tests on Bluebook for full-length test simulation every 2 weeks

Khan teaches you the material. Daily practice makes sure you remember it when it counts.

Bottom Line

Khan Academy is still a solid free resource, especially for learning concepts. But if you've been using Khan alone and your scores aren't budging, the problem probably isn't the content. It's the consistency of your practice.

Adding a quick daily practice tool to your routine is the single highest-leverage change most students can make.
