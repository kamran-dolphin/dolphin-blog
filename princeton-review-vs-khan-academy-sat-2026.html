Princeton Review vs Khan Academy for SAT 2026: Which to Choose?

Princeton Review and Khan Academy sit at opposite ends of the SAT prep world. Princeton Review is premium, structured, with live classes and score guarantees. Khan Academy is free, self-paced, and officially partnered with the College Board.

Both have been around forever. But the SAT itself has changed a lot. Here's how they stack up for the Digital SAT in 2026.

Cost

The most obvious difference. Princeton Review runs from $499 for self-paced to $2,400+ for private tutoring. Khan Academy is completely free. No upsells, no premium tier, no hidden anything.

For families watching their budget, this might be the only comparison that matters.

Structure vs. Flexibility

Princeton Review gives you a schedule: show up at this time, follow this plan, a real instructor walks you through it. For students who need someone holding them accountable (and that's most teenagers, honestly), this works.

Khan Academy is totally self-directed. You pick what to study, when, and how much. The platform suggests things based on your diagnostic, but nobody's checking in on you. Works great for the naturally disciplined. Can be a struggle for everyone else.

Question Quality

Both are solid here. Khan has the edge in official alignment since they're the College Board's partner. Princeton Review's questions are proprietary and well-made, though some students say they feel slightly different from the real test.

The Score Guarantee

Princeton Review offers a score improvement guarantee: if your score doesn't go up, you get your money back or a free retake. Sounds great, but check the fine print. You usually need to complete every assignment and attend every session to qualify.

Khan offers no guarantee. But it's also free, so there's zero financial risk.

What Both Miss: Daily Mobile Practice

Here's the gap neither platform fills well. Neither was designed for how students actually use their phones in 2026: quick, frequent bursts throughout the day.

Princeton Review's value is in live sessions. Between sessions, you're on your own. Khan offers anytime practice, but the experience is built for focused desktop sessions, not a quick 5 minutes between classes.

This is why a lot of students supplement both with mobile-first apps. Dolphin delivers SAT questions in a swipeable feed that works the same way TikTok does. Sessions take 2 minutes. You can practice during dead time that would otherwise go to scrolling Instagram.

The data backs this up: students who practice daily, even briefly, consistently outperform those who only study during scheduled sessions. Regardless of which platform those sessions are on.

Which Should You Choose?

Princeton Review if: You have the budget, need external accountability, and want a human instructor guiding your prep.

Khan Academy if: You're budget-conscious, self-motivated, and disciplined enough to follow your own schedule.

The ideal combo: Khan or Princeton Review for core learning, a daily practice app like Dolphin for retention, and Bluebook for full-length test simulations.

The Real Secret

The platform matters less than you think. A student who practices 15 minutes daily on a free app will probably outscore someone who paid $2,000 for Princeton Review but only studies during class time. The best prep plan is the one you actually stick to every day.
